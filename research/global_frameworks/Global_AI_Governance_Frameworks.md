# Global AI Governance Frameworks: EU AI Act and NIST AI RMF

## Introduction

The rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has necessitated the development of robust governance frameworks to ensure its ethical, safe, and responsible deployment. As AI systems become increasingly integrated into various sectors, regulatory bodies worldwide are striving to establish guidelines that address potential risks while fostering innovation. This document provides a comprehensive overview of two prominent global AI governance frameworks: the European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act) and the National Institute of Standards and Technology's AI Risk Management Framework (NIST AI RMF).

## The European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act)

The EU AI Act, formally known as Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, stands as the world's first comprehensive legal framework for AI. It aims to foster trustworthy AI within Europe by setting out a risk-based approach to regulate AI developers and deployers [1]. The Act entered into force on August 1, 2024, with various provisions becoming applicable in phases through 2025 and 2026 [2].

### Risk-Based Approach

The EU AI Act categorizes AI systems into four levels of risk, each with corresponding obligations:

*   **Unacceptable Risk:** AI systems considered a clear threat to the safety, livelihoods, and rights of individuals are banned. This includes practices such as harmful AI-based manipulation, social scoring, and untargeted scraping of internet or CCTV material for facial recognition databases [1]. These prohibitions became effective in February 2025 [1].

*   **High Risk:** AI use cases that pose serious risks to health, safety, or fundamental rights are classified as high-risk. Examples include AI safety components in critical infrastructure, AI solutions in education and employment, and AI systems used in law enforcement. Providers of high-risk AI systems are subject to strict obligations, including adequate risk assessment and mitigation systems, high-quality datasets, logging of activity, detailed documentation, human oversight, and high levels of robustness, cybersecurity, and accuracy [1]. The main enforcement date for high-risk systems is August 2, 2026 [4].

*   **Transparency Risk:** This category addresses the need for transparency in AI use. The Act mandates specific disclosure obligations, such as informing users when they are interacting with an AI system (e.g., chatbots) and requiring providers of generative AI to ensure that AI-generated content is identifiable. Deepfakes and text published to inform the public on matters of public interest must be clearly and visibly labeled [1]. These transparency rules will come into effect in August 2026 [1].

*   **Minimal or No Risk:** The vast majority of AI systems, such as AI-enabled video games or spam filters, fall into this category and are not subject to specific legal obligations under the Act. However, voluntary compliance codes are encouraged [4].

### General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Models

The AI Act also includes rules for General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models, which can perform a wide range of tasks and form the basis for many AI systems. Providers of GPAI models, especially those with systemic risks, must comply with transparency and copyright-related rules, and assess and mitigate potential risks. Obligations for GPAI providers became effective in August 2025 [1].

### Compliance and Enforcement

The EU AI Act has extraterritorial reach, meaning it applies to any organization placing AI systems on the EU market or deploying AI that affects EU residents, regardless of their location [4]. Penalties for serious violations can be substantial, reaching up to 7% of annual global turnover or €35 million, whichever is higher [4]. The EU AI Office is operational, and member states are designating supervisory authorities to ensure enforcement [4].

## The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) is a voluntary, non-sector-specific framework designed to help organizations manage the risks associated with AI systems [3]. Published in January 2023, the AI RMF aims to integrate risk management into the design, development, deployment, and use of AI products and services [3] [5].

### Core Functions

The AI RMF is structured around four core functions, which provide a systematic approach to managing AI risks:

*   **Govern:** Establish a culture of risk management, including policies, procedures, and structures for managing AI risks. This involves defining roles and responsibilities, promoting transparency, and ensuring accountability [5].

*   **Map:** Identify and characterize AI risks, including potential harms, vulnerabilities, and threats. This function emphasizes understanding the context of AI use, identifying stakeholders, and assessing potential impacts [5].

*   **Measure:** Evaluate, analyze, and track AI risks. This involves developing metrics, conducting assessments, and monitoring the effectiveness of risk mitigation strategies [5].

*   **Manage:** Prioritize, respond to, and recover from AI risks. This includes implementing controls, developing response plans, and continuously improving risk management processes [5].

### Principles and Characteristics

The AI RMF is built upon several key principles, including fairness, accountability, transparency, and explainability. It emphasizes a flexible and adaptable approach, allowing organizations to tailor the framework to their specific needs and contexts. NIST continues to align the AI RMF with applicable international standards, guidelines, and practices [3].

### Voluntary Adoption and Impact

While the NIST AI RMF is voluntary, it has become a widely recognized guide for AI governance. Many organizations, particularly in the Fortune 500, are demanding adherence to frameworks like the AI RMF as part of their procurement processes, and insurance underwriters are increasingly requiring documented compliance for AI systems [4].

## Comparative Analysis and Interoperability

Both the EU AI Act and the NIST AI RMF aim to promote responsible AI, but they differ in their approach. The EU AI Act is a legally binding regulation with a strong focus on compliance and enforcement, particularly for high-risk AI systems. In contrast, the NIST AI RMF is a voluntary framework that provides guidance for risk management, emphasizing flexibility and adaptability.

Despite their differences, these frameworks are not mutually exclusive and can complement each other. Organizations operating globally may need to comply with the EU AI Act's mandatory requirements while also adopting the NIST AI RMF's best practices for comprehensive risk management. The NIST AI RMF's focus on continuous improvement and adaptability can help organizations build robust internal processes that support compliance with regulations like the EU AI Act.

## Conclusion

The landscape of AI governance is rapidly evolving, with frameworks like the EU AI Act and NIST AI RMF leading the way in shaping responsible AI development and deployment. Understanding these frameworks is crucial for businesses and AI developers seeking to navigate the complex regulatory environment and ensure the ethical and safe use of AI. As AI technology continues to advance, ongoing efforts to harmonize and adapt these governance structures will be essential for fostering a global ecosystem of trustworthy AI.
